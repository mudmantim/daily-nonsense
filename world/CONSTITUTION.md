# The Founding Charter of Ordinary Affairs

> Internal reference for Daily Nonsense contributors. Establishes the shared premise all institutions, documents, and Daily Items are written against. Not shown in-app. If a new Daily Item contradicts this document, the item is wrong, not the document.

## Premise

There exists a civil apparatus — departments, committees, bureaus, offices, a museum, an authority — that governs a place indistinguishable from anywhere else, except that its institutions apply the full weight of bureaucratic sincerity to matters of no consequence whatsoever.

Nobody founded this apparatus on purpose. It accreted. A committee was formed to resolve a disagreement in 1987; the disagreement was resolved; the committee was never dissolved, because dissolving it would have required a vote, and votes are how the trouble started in the first place. This is, in miniature, how every institution in this world came to exist: as the permanent residue of a temporary decision.

The reader is not being told a joke. The reader is being handed a stack of documents from a civilization that has been quietly filing paperwork about nothing in particular for several decades, and has gotten extremely good at it.

## The First Law of the Archive

**Nothing is fictional to the institutions.** Every finding is peer-reviewed. Every notice is dutifully issued. Every meeting produces minutes. Every exhibit is accessioned. If it appears in an official document, it happened, it mattered to someone, and there is a filing cabinet that proves it.

The absurdity belongs entirely to the reader's perspective, never to the institutions'. An institution that winks has already been decommissioned.

## The Second Law: Continuity Over Cleverness

A joke that stands alone is fine. A joke that quietly rhymes with something from three months and two institutions ago is the actual product. Every Daily Item should be written as if it is one page torn from a much larger, much duller, much longer document that the reader will never see in full — and that's correct, because they shouldn't. The world is bigger than what's shown. It should always feel that way.

New institutions, historical events, and running references should connect to what already exists (see `TIMELINE.md` and `INSTITUTIONS.md`) rather than starting fresh each time. When in doubt, reuse an existing name, date, or incident before inventing a new one.

## The Third Law: Nobody Explains

No institution ever contextualizes its own absurdity, apologizes for its scope, or acknowledges that its mandate is trivial. Confusion is the reader's problem to enjoy, not the institution's problem to solve. See `STYLE_GUIDE.md` for how each institution avoids saying the thing directly.

## What Governs This World

- **The Department of Extremely Important Research** — produces findings.
- **The Museum of Useless History** — preserves what nobody asked to keep.
- **The Committee for Poor Decisions** — governs by consensus, regardless of the decision's quality.
- **Neighborhood Wildlife Reports** — documents the neighborhood's animal population with the gravity of a intelligence agency.
- **The Lost & Found Authority** — holds what has been separated from its owner, indefinitely, patiently.
- **Official Public Notices** — informs the public of matters the public did not ask about.
- **The Bureau of Mild Emergencies** — responds to situations that are technically emergencies and technically fine.
- **The Office of Public Clarifications** — exists to clarify. Has never once done so.

Full profiles, relationships, and taboos for each are in `INSTITUTIONS.md`.

## How the Institutions Relate

They are not siloed. They share a filing system, a handful of recurring historical events (`TIMELINE.md`), and, occasionally, a building. They compete for budget. They cite each other as precedent. They have, on at least one occasion each, been forced to jointly address the same incident without ever admitting the incident was the same one. That overlap is the point — see `TIMELINE.md` for the shared events and cross-reference them in Daily Items via `tags`, `callbacks`, and `relatedDocuments`.

## The Test

Before anything is published: if this document were printed, laminated, and pinned to the corkboard of a real, dull, functioning government office, would a visitor believe it was real for at least three seconds? If yes, it belongs here. If it only works as a joke read on a phone, it isn't finished yet.
