# DEIR Procedural Manual: From Hypothesis to Published Finding

> In-universe document. Ref: `DEIR-PM-0002`.

## Step 1: Hypothesis Intake (Form DEIR-H1)

Any staff member may submit a hypothesis regarding any subject, provided it is stated with appropriate uncertainty. Hypotheses stated with excessive confidence are returned for revision, as confidence prior to peer review is considered a methodological red flag.

## Step 2: Study Design

The study must include:
- a sample size the Department considers "sufficient," a term the Department has never formally defined;
- a control group, wherever a control group is conceptually possible;
- a note acknowledging what the study does not address, filed separately as an Appendix of Acknowledged Gaps.

## Step 3: Data Collection

Data may be collected by observation, survey, or — where the subject matter permits — by simply asking someone and writing down what they said. All three methods are considered equally rigorous provided the results are reported with the same tone of institutional confidence.

## Step 4: Peer Review (Form DEIR-PR1)

See Employee Handbook §2. If no reviewer is available, the finding proceeds with the note "researchers were unavailable for comment," which appears in the historical record with sufficient frequency that new staff should not read it as unusual.

## Step 5: Classification

Every finding is classified upon publication (see `SEALS_AND_SIGNATURES.md` and `deir`'s metadata field, "Classification: Public Curiosity"). Findings are never classified as "trivial." Public Curiosity is the only classification currently in use.

## Step 6: Publication

The finding is assigned a reference number per `FILING_SYSTEM.md` (`DEIR-[code]-####`) and published under one of the Department's recurring formats: New Study Finds, Research Finding, Scientific Paper, Press Release, or Safety Bulletin.

## Step 7: Continued Funding

Every published finding closes with a note that further funding has been requested. This is not optional. A finding without a funding request is considered institutionally incomplete, regardless of its scientific merit.
